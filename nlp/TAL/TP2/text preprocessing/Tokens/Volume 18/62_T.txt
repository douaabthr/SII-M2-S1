<s>
Beyond
publication
numbers
a
novel
approach
to
academic
ranking
using
evolutionary
programming
</s>
<s>
Evaluating
the
impact
of
researchers
within
a
scientific
community
remains
a
complex
and
debated
issue
</s>
<s>
Traditional
metrics
such
as
publication
and
citation
counts
often
fail
to
capture
the
multifaceted
nature
of
scholarly
influence
</s>
<s>
While
several
indices
such
as
the
h-index
and
its
variants
have
been
proposed
they
too
face
limitations
in
reflecting
qualitative
and
longitudinal
aspects
of
academic
contributions
</s>
<s>
This
study
introduces
a
novel
researcher
ranking
framework
that
integrates
four
distinct
parameters
Normalized
Citation
Score
NCS
Complete
Career
Contribution
CCC
Temporal
Citation
Input
TCI
and
Collaborative
Prestige
CP
</s>
<s>
These
parameters
are
combined
using
two
ranking
mechanisms
the
Statistical
Ranking
System
SRS
based
on
mathematical
models
and
the
Comprehensive
Ranking
System
CRS
which
employs
Genetic
Programming
GP
to
evolve
domain-specific
ranking
formulas
</s>
<s>
The
methodology
was
evaluated
across
five
datasets
Computer
Science
Mathematics
Neuroscience
Civil
Engineering
and
a
combined
dataset
each
balanced
with
equal
numbers
of
awardees
and
non-awardees
</s>
<s>
In
SRS
models
such
as
the
Lehmer
Mean
and
Logarithmic
Mean
performed
effectively
in
highlighting
awardees
</s>
<s>
In
CRS
the
evolved
models
achieved
fitness
values
of
up
to
0.96
in
Computer
Science
and
0.88
in
Mathematics
with
slightly
lower
scores
in
other
domains
</s>
<s>
The
reduced
performance
on
the
combined
dataset
highlights
the
importance
of
domain-sensitive
modeling
</s>
<s>
The
results
suggest
that
the
proposed
framework
offers
a
flexible
and
comprehensive
approach
to
researcher
evaluation
that
can
adapt
to
domain-specific
impact
patterns
providing
an
alternative
to
conventional
ranking
metrics
.
</s>
